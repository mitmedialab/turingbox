The rapid growth of artificial intelligence has hindered our ability to replicate and audit the behavior of emerging AI algorithms. These hindrances have opened the door to unintended consequences like unexpected biases, unethical behavior, and unequal access to these algorithms.

TuringBox is a two-sided marketplace that facilitates both the behavioural study of artificial intelligence algorithms and the production of new, socially-aware systems.

On one side of the market, examiners can study the behavior of algorithms by creating boxes, which are AI algorithms paired with datasets that create an observable environment for the algorithm. On the other side contributors can upload existing and novel artificial intelligence algorithms to be benchmarked and to solve boxes to score points.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is machine behavior?

Machine behavior is the quantified measurement of the output of a black box AI algorithm, particular in the context of important and high-stakes systems. You are probably already familiar with machine behavior in the form of algorithmic bias or with algorithms which cooperate with people.

2. Where does the name "TuringBox" come from?

The name "TuringBox" hearkens back to two influential scientists: Alan Turing, the father of artificial intelligence, and B.F. Skinner, a behaviorist and inventor of the "operant conditioning chamber" also known as the "Skinner Box." The Skinner Box is a laboratory tool which fundamentally changed the study of animal behavior by allowing scientists to study an animal's behavior in response to very controlled stimuli. Just as the Skinner Box allowed for the study of animal behavior in a controlled environment, TuringBox now allows scientists to study at scale the behavior of artificial intelligence algorithms in a controlled environment.

3. What is a box?

A box is a fundamental unit of TuringBox. It constitutes an input (data), AI algorithms, and a way of measuring them. As an examiner, you can create a box to study the AI algorithms.
